How film production exploded across 14 decades — and which genres rose with it.

The exponential curve, anchored to three industry inflection points.

2010s alone produced 7,360 films — about 1.7× the entire 1870-1970 century combined.

Share evolution across the eight dominant genres — each decade sums to 100%. Decades with fewer than 50 films are omitted to avoid sampling noise.

Drama has led every decade since 1920; Documentary's share grew 66% from 2000s (4.7%) to 2010s (7.8%).

Each row sums to 100% — relative dominance per decade. Pre-1900s decades have only 1-3 films, so 100% Documentary there is a small-sample artifact.

Drama's dominance peaked in the 1990s at ~30%; Comedy and Documentary keep eating its share in the modern era.

Money in vs. money out — what the {{ "{:,}".format(kpis.disclosed_count) }} disclosed films tell us about the economics of cinema.

Log-log scatter with regression line. Slope = price elasticity (≠ a "$X per $1" multiplier).

The median film returns $2.12 per $1 of budget; log-log elasticity 0.86 means revenue grows slightly slower than budget — diminishing returns at the high end.

ROI distribution per genre, log scale. Filtered to films that at least broke even (1× ≤ ROI < 50×) so the chart shows successful-film economics, not loss-making outliers.

Among films that broke even, Horror leads at 3.55× median return, edging Animation (3.37×) and Drama (3.18×) — micro-budget genres extract the most leverage.

Pearson correlation matrix on disclosed-financial films. Cells with |r| < 0.05 dimmed as effectively zero.

Revenue and Profit move in lockstep (r=0.98); ROI is uncorrelated with everything — small films can still win huge.

How TMDB users rate films across history, genre, and the long tail of taste.

Histogram of TMDB vote averages, 0.5-point bins. Red = median, yellow = mean — the gap reveals which way ratings lean.

Ratings cluster sharply around 6.0–6.5 (peak: 4,556 films) with a long tail toward low scores — TMDB users rate generously, harshly only when they really mean it.

Mean rating per decade, films with ≥10 votes; decades with <20 films excluded. Marker size ∝ log(sample) so the modern-era confidence is visible.

Modern films hover steadily around 6.0–6.3; older films skew higher (1920s peak: 7.03) — survival bias of classics that survived to be rated.

Rating distribution per genre, 19,083 films with ≥10 votes. Genres sorted by median rating, descending.

Documentary tops at 7.10 median; Horror sits last at 5.40 — beloved at the box office (highest ROI in Q2), not the heart.
